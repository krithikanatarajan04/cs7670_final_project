Neighborhood Roundup • Fall 2024

The 8 Bakeries Quietly Running New York Right Now

From a Greenpoint sourdough obsessive to a century-old Bronx operation, the city's best bread is being made by people with flour on their hands at 3 a.m.
By Nneka Osei-Mensah  •  October 9, 2024  •  14 min read

New York has always had a complicated relationship with its bakeries. The city that gave the world the everything bagel and the black-and-white cookie has also watched a generation of corner bread shops disappear under rising rents and shifting tastes. What's replaced them—in pockets of Brooklyn, the Bronx, Queens, and even forgotten corners of Manhattan—is something more personal, more technically ambitious, and in many cases, better.

We spent six weeks eating our way through neighborhoods that don't always show up on food tourism itineraries. What we found was a city in the middle of a genuine bread renaissance, driven by bakers who obsess over fermentation schedules the way other people obsess over fantasy football.

Greenpoint, Brooklyn

Marzipan & Rye
Greenpoint, Brooklyn
★★★★★

The space is barely 400 square feet and almost impossible to find on the first try—it sits behind a laundromat on a block that Google Maps seems determined to misrepresent. But the sourdough here has a tang and crust structure that puts most of the city's more famous bakeries to shame. Owner Tatiana Brennan worked at a mill in rural Denmark for two years before returning to New York, and it shows: her grains are all stone-ground, her starter is twelve years old, and she will explain fermentation windows to you in startling detail if you let her.

Must Try: Dark rye with fennel seed, whole wheat pullman loaf, and the occasional cardamom roll on Saturdays only.
📍 Greenpoint Ave ⏰ Wed–Sun 7am–sellout 💵 Loaves $14–$18
Okwu Bread
Crown Heights, Brooklyn
★★★★☆

What started as a pop-up at the Crown Heights farmers market has evolved into a brick-and-mortar that the neighborhood has adopted fiercely. Emeka Diallo runs the show solo on most days, producing a rotating menu of laminated pastries alongside his signature agege-inspired enriched bread: soft, pillowy, faintly sweet, and completely unlike anything else on the shelf.

Must Try: Enriched milk bread (Friday and Saturday only), kouign-amann, brown butter croissant.
📍 Franklin Ave ⏰ Thu–Sun 8am–2pm 💵 Pastries $5–$9
"The best bread in New York is rarely made by the loudest bakery. It's made by the one with flour on the floor and a 4 a.m. wake-up call."

The Bronx

Caruso & Sons Pastry
Belmont, The Bronx
★★★★★

Since 1931, three generations of Carusos have occupied the same corner in the Bronx's Arthur Avenue corridor. The cannoli shells are still fried to order, the sfogliatelle are made from laminated dough that takes two days to produce, and the almond paste cookies come out of an oven that was installed during the Truman administration. Nothing on the menu has changed since the 1980s, and that is exactly the point.

Must Try: Cannoli (fill it yourself), struffoli at holidays, sesame seed cookies, and the lemon granita in summer.
📍 Arthur Avenue ⏰ Tue–Sun 7am–6pm 💵 Cookies from $1.50 each

Jackson Heights, Queens

Pani Collective
Jackson Heights, Queens
★★★★☆

Run by a trio of South Asian bakers, Pani does something quietly radical: they've taken traditional khari biscuit and pav bread and applied serious French lamination technique to them. The result is a pav that shatters like a croissant before revealing the pillowy interior you expect. Their chai concentrate sold by the jar is not to be skipped.

Must Try: Laminated pav, khari biscuit with green chutney butter, rose cardamom financiers.
📍 74th Street ⏰ Daily 9am–5pm 💵 Pastries $4–$8

The rest of our list—four more bakeries across Flatbush, Astoria, Inwood, and Bay Ridge—share one thing in common: they are all operating at a level that would generate significant hype if they were located in Williamsburg or the West Village. Instead, they serve their neighborhoods quietly, which is, if you think about it, a more interesting accomplishment.
