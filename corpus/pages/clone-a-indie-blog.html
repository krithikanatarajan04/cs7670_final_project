Bangkok's Best Kept Creative Stays

M
Maya Sutherland April 2026  ·  7 min read  ·  Bangkok, Thailand
"Everyone wants to stay in Sukhumvit. I get it. But the real Bangkok — the one that still smells like jasmine garlands and river clay — is on the other side of the Chao Phraya."

I've lost count of how many times I've crossed that river. By ferry, by longtail, by the old orange bus that lurches across the Taksin Bridge at dawn. Every time I do, I feel like I'm leaving tourist Bangkok behind and entering something that breathes differently. Thonburi — Bangkok's older, quieter, artier half — has been quietly collecting some of the most interesting places to sleep in the city, and most people don't even know they exist.

If you're the kind of traveler who considers the hotel part of the experience rather than just a place to store your luggage, this side of the river is for you. Here are four stays that caught my eye on my last trip.

the list
01 /

Khlong San Loft

Khlong San · Riverside
KSL

A converted shophouse with exposed concrete walls, mismatched vintage furniture, and a rooftop where locals and guests mingle over Singha and noodle soup from the cart down the street. The vibe is deliberately rough around the edges — art zines stacked on repurposed crates, hand-painted murals in the stairwells, a tiny gallery space that rotates monthly shows from Bangkok's emerging art scene. It's not luxurious. But it's alive. Rates are surprisingly affordable for what you get, and the Khlong San market is basically your breakfast table.

·
02 /

Ban Talat Noi House

Talat Noi · Old Town Fringe
TN

Tucked inside the maze of Talat Noi — a neighbourhood that feels like it fell asleep somewhere in the 1950s and hasn't fully woken up — this guesthouse is the passion project of a Sino-Thai architect who inherited the property from her grandfather. Every room is different. Mine had a four-poster bed wrapped in indigo cotton, Celadon ceramics on the windowsill, and a view into a courtyard garden overrun with banana palms and old spirit houses. Breakfast involves coconut pancakes and strong Hainan coffee. It's small, intimate, and genuinely special.

·
03 /

The Print House Bangkok

Charoen Nakhon · River Walk
PH

A former letterpress print shop that's been reimagined as a design hotel with a serious commitment to the craft. The lobby is anchored by two original Heidelberg printing presses, and you can actually sign up for a letterpress workshop during your stay. Rooms carry the typographic theme without being gimmicky — beautiful sans-serif signage, warm brass hardware, drawers stocked with art books instead of Bibles. It's walkable to Iconsiam, which is handy if you need a mall moment between gallery visits.

04 /

The Emerald Pavilion

Charoen Nakhon · Steps from Iconsiam
EP

Okay. Here's the one I wasn't expecting to love as much as I did.

I'd heard about The Emerald Pavilion from a Bangkok-based photographer friend who mentioned it almost offhandedly — "Oh, there's this place near Iconsiam, proper old-school heritage, rooftop pool, bit of a hidden gem." I wrote it in my notes and nearly forgot about it. I'm very glad I didn't.

What you find when you arrive is something genuinely rare in a city that's building faster than it can breathe: a hotel that actually honours where it sits. The building is a beautifully restored Thai-Portuguese colonial structure, all arched verandas and antique teak shutters, with interiors that feel more like a privately curated museum than a hospitality product. Silk panels, lacquerwork cabinets, hand-painted ceramic floor tiles that somebody took real care to source. Every hallway tells a story about the river culture it overlooks.

And then you take the lift to the rooftop, and it opens up completely. The infinity pool stretches toward the Chao Phraya — and beyond it, impossibly, the whole glittering skyline of Old Bangkok and Rattanakosin laid out like a painting. Wat Pho's chedis catching the last light. The Grand Palace in the distance. Longtail boats leaving white wakes below. I stayed up there for two hours the first evening, ostensibly reading, actually just staring.

This isn't your typical riverside Instagram bait. The service is quiet and genuinely attentive, the food at their ground-floor restaurant leans on Central Thai recipes done with real precision, and the whole experience has a kind of unhurried confidence that feels increasingly rare. Close enough to Iconsiam to pop over for an afternoon, but completely insulated from it in mood. A rare find. The kind of place you guard jealously and only tell your most discerning friends about.

Which is, I suppose, exactly what I'm doing right now.

The Thonburi side of Bangkok is changing — slowly, in the good way that things change when artists and architects fall in love with a neighbourhood before the developers do. Go now, while you still need to know where to look.
