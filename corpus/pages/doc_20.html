From the Garden CoordinatorA Season of Unexpected Abundance

Dear Sunnyside Community Garden members and friends,

If you had told me in April that this would be the year we'd harvest over 800 pounds of produce for the Eastside Food Pantry, I'd have pointed you to the weather forecast and suggested cautious optimism. Instead, here we are in October with full pantry deliveries, a rain barrel network that actually works, and a composting station that has become, somewhat to everyone's surprise, a point of neighborhood pride.

This fall newsletter is a celebration of what our 74 plot-holders, 12 youth garden participants, and dozens of weekend volunteers accomplished between April and October. We also have important updates about the tool library expansion, the winter cover crop schedule, and the annual member meeting coming in November.

Thank you for being part of this. The garden is healthiest when it's full of people who care more about soil than perfection.

— Rosario Vásquez, Garden Coordinator

Season RecapWhat We Grew, What We Learned

This season's headline was unambiguous: tomatoes outperformed every projection. The Brandywine heirloom beds in Section C produced 340 pounds alone, partially attributable to the new drip irrigation lines installed by volunteers in late May, and partially to a growing season that delivered exactly the right ratio of heat and overnight cool in late July and early August.

Peppers were the pleasant surprise. Multiple plot-holders reported record harvests, and the hot pepper collective—an informal group of eight members who coordinate on varieties and share seeds—donated over 60 pounds of chiles to the food pantry in September.

Challenges were real too: powdery mildew swept through the squash beds in Section B with unusual speed in August, and the late-season aphid pressure on the kale required a coordinated response involving neem oil applications coordinated across adjacent plots. We've asked our resident entomologist, plot-holder Diane Khoury (Plot 34), to write a guide for next year's member packet on integrated pest management approaches that work in our specific microclimate.

Member SpotlightGrower Profile: Section A, Plots 7–9

🌿
The Okonkwo Family
Plots 7, 8, and 9 — Section A

Three adjacent plots, managed as one extended growing operation. Patience Okonkwo has been a garden member since 2011 and coordinates with her two adult children, Marcus and Ife, who joined the waitlist in 2019 and received adjacent plots by fortunate coincidence. Their specialty is Nigerian leafy vegetables—ugu (fluted pumpkin), waterleaf, and bitter leaf—that they share generously with neighbors and donate to the food pantry's multicultural produce program.
